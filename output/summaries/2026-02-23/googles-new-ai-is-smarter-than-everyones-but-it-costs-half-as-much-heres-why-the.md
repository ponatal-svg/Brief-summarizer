# Google's New AI Is Smarter Than Everyone's But It Costs HALF as Much. Here's Why They Don't Care.

## The Hook
Google has released Gemini 3.1 Pro, which the presenter claims is the "smartest AI model on the planet," leading on 13 of 16 benchmarks and costing roughly one-seventh of Opus 4.6. The presenter states that Google's strategy behind this release, and their apparent indifference to its immediate adoption, is the "most important strategic signal in AI right now."

## Key Findings
*   Gemini 3.1 Pro achieved a 77.1% score on the ARC AGI2 benchmark, a "46 percentage point jump" from Gemini 3 Pro's 31.1% just 90 days prior, which the presenter describes as the "largest single generation reasoning gain any Frontier model family has ever produced." Opus 4.6 scored 68.8% on the same benchmark. [t=155s]
*   According to the presenter, Google optimized Gemini 3.1 Pro for "pure reasoning," designed to "think harder" and "reason more deeply about problems it has never seen." This contrasts with Anthropic building Opus 4.6 for "agentic work" and OpenAI building Codex 5.3 for "specialized coding pipelines." The presenter states that Demis Hassabis's mission for Google is "Step one, solve intelligence. Step two, use it to solve everything else." [t=218s]
*   Google possesses a unique "vertical stack in AI," encompassing self-designed silicon like the 7th generation Ironwood TPU, which provides "10 times the compute power of the last generation at roughly half the energy cost per operation." They train their models on this hardware, deploy through "Google Cloud" (used by "nine out of 10 AI research labs"), and distribute to "650 million monthly active Gemini users" plus billions more through other Google products. This is funded by "over a hundred billion dollars in annual free cash flow" from search, YouTube, and cloud, allowing Gemini to function as a "research vehicle." [t=407s]
*   Gemini 3.1 Pro is positioned as cost-efficient, being "roughly seven and a half times cheaper than Opus 4.6 on input" and "more than six times cheaper on output," priced at "$2 per million input tokens" and "$12 per million output tokens." It also features configurable thinking levels—low, medium, high, and max—to adjust reasoning depth and cost for different tasks. [t=594s]
*   The presenter differentiates between various types of "hard" problems: pure reasoning (where Gemini 3.1 Pro excels, exemplified by Deepthink solving 18 previously unsolved problems), effort problems (where agentic models like Opus 4.6 perform strongly), coordination problems, emotional intelligence problems, judgment/willpower problems, domain expertise problems, and ambiguity problems. The presenter notes that "pure reasoning problems do exist in mainstream business" but are "rarer and much more specialized" than typically assumed. [t=1000s]

## The So What?
The presenter concludes that the pertinent question is no longer "which AI should I use," but rather "which AI should I use for which problem?" and understanding the specific type of problem being addressed. He states that models have become sufficiently differentiated to reward specific "task to model mapping," asserting that individuals who effectively route tasks to the appropriate model will "way outperform" those using a single model for everything, a performance gap expected to "widen every single month." According to the presenter, Google is focusing on "the thing underneath the thing," prioritizing intelligence itself, rather than the immediate product race.
